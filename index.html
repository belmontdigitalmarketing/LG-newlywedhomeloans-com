Conventional Loans

Traditional mortgages with competitive rates. As little as 3% down for first-time buyers with good credit.

FHA Loans

Government-backed loans with just 3.5% down and flexible credit requirements — ideal for first-time buyers.

Buying Your First Home as Newlyweds

Getting married is exciting — and so is the prospect of owning your first home together. But navigating the mortgage process as a newly married couple comes with unique considerations.

Should you use both incomes to qualify? What if one spouse has student loan debt? How do you decide between renting longer and buying now? These are all questions we help couples answer every day.

As newlyweds, you may have access to first-time homebuyer programs that offer lower down payments, reduced interest rates, and even down payment assistance grants. Many couples are surprised to learn they can afford more home than they expected — or that they can buy sooner than they thought.

We connect you with lenders who specialize in working with young couples and first-time buyers, so you get expert guidance tailored to your situation.

Yes! One of the biggest advantages of buying as a married couple is combining both incomes on the mortgage application. This can help you qualify for a larger loan amount and better interest rates. Both incomes are counted when calculating your debt-to-income ratio.

Down payment requirements vary by loan type. Conventional loans typically require 3-20%, FHA loans require as little as 3.5%, VA loans (if eligible) require 0% down, and USDA loans in eligible rural areas also offer 0% down options. Many state programs also offer down payment assistance for first-time buyers.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required on conventional loans when your down payment is less than 20%. It protects the lender if you default. You can avoid PMI by putting 20% down, choosing a VA or USDA loan (which don't require PMI), or using lender-paid PMI options where the cost is built into your rate.

Not necessarily. If one spouse has poor credit or high debt, it may be better to apply with only the stronger borrower to get a better rate. However, using both incomes allows you to qualify for more. A loan officer can run scenarios both ways to help you decide.

Minimum credit scores vary by loan type: FHA loans require 580+ (or 500 with 10% down), conventional loans typically require 620+, and VA loans have no official minimum but lenders usually want 620+. Higher scores qualify for better interest rates, which can save you tens of thousands over the life of the loan.
